100 Questions to Ask Your Best Friend

Here's the thing nobody admits: the questions aren't the point. The point is the gap — the space between what your friends are sure they know about you and what they actually know. That gap is where all the fun lives, and you only find it by asking things that have a real answer. A question like "what makes me happy?" is lovely and completely useless, because everyone passes it. The questions that work are the ones with a specific, checkable answer: the exact drink you order, the film you've rewatched eleven times, the thing you're quietly proud of. A day-one friend names it instantly. An acquaintance guesses something reasonable and gets it wrong. That's the whole game. Below are 100 questions built to do that, written in your voice — the person being guessed about.

1. The easy favourites

Start here. These are the warm-up round, and they matter more than they look — they're how you find out whether someone has been paying attention to the surface of your life at all. Anyone who has eaten a meal with you should clear most of these. The trick is to make them specific enough to be wrong: not "my favourite food" but "the meal I order every single time at the place we always go". Vague questions let people guess; specific ones make them remember.

  1. What's my go-to order at a coffee shop, down to the milk?
  2. What food do I claim to love and then never actually order?
  3. Which film have I made you watch against your will?
  4. What colour do I say is my favourite, and what colour do I actually own forty items of?
  5. Which song do I replay three times in a row before letting the album continue?
  6. What's the one snack that disappears from my kitchen fastest?
  7. If I'm ordering pizza, what topping is non-negotiable?
  8. What's my comfort show — the one I put on when I'm not really watching?
  9. Which fast food place would I defend in public?
  10. What drink am I holding at 11pm at a party?
  11. What's my favourite meal of the day, and don't say the obvious one?
  12. Which season do I genuinely love and which one do I just tolerate?
  13. What's the one condiment I put on things that horrify people?

2. Habits and quirks

This category is where friendships get separated from acquaintanceships. Nobody learns your habits from a conversation — they learn them from being in the room, in the car, on the group trip. These questions reward time spent together, which is exactly why they're so satisfying to get right. Expect people who've known you for a decade to nail these and people who've known you for a year to fall apart.

  1. How many alarms do I set, and how many do I actually use?
  2. What do I do with my hands when I'm nervous?
  3. Am I early, exactly on time, or the person everyone lies to about the start time?
  4. What's the first thing I do when I walk into my own house?
  5. What phrase do I say so often you could set a clock by it?
  6. Do I read the menu before I get to the restaurant?
  7. Which chore do I actually enjoy, and which one sits undone for weeks?
  8. How do I hold my phone when I'm texting — one thumb, two, or the weird way?
  9. What's my move when a conversation gets awkward?
  10. Do I make the bed, and am I lying about it?
  11. What's in my bag that has no business being in my bag?
  12. How long do I take to reply to a text I don't want to answer?
  13. Which app do I open by accident when I meant to open a different one?

3. The deep cuts

These are the ones you'd never volunteer over dinner. Fears, regrets, the private pride you'd deflect a compliment about. They land hardest and they can also land badly, so read the room — ask these of the people who've earned them. When someone guesses one of these right, it's genuinely moving, because it means they noticed something you never said out loud. Use two or three of these in a quiz, not ten.

  1. What am I quietly proud of that I'd never bring up myself?
  2. What's the compliment I don't know how to accept?
  3. Which decision do I still turn over at 2am?
  4. What am I more afraid of than I let on?
  5. What did I want to be at ten years old, and how far did I get?
  6. What's the thing I'd change about myself if it were free?
  7. Which person shaped me most, for better or worse?
  8. What do I pretend not to care about?
  9. What's the last thing that genuinely made me cry?
  10. When I say "I'm fine", what am I usually actually feeling?
  11. What's the apology I still owe someone?
  12. What do I think people get wrong about me on first meeting?
  13. Which of my achievements do I undersell the most?

4. Would you rather

Forced choices are a shortcut. Ask someone directly what they value and you'll get a thoughtful, careful, useless answer. Make them pick between two things and their actual priorities fall out instantly. These are also the most fun to argue about afterwards, which is half the reason to include them. Pick the ones where you already know your own answer without hesitating.

  1. Would I rather be famous for something embarrassing or unknown for something great?
  2. Would I rather lose all my photos or all my messages?
  3. Would I rather have a job I love that pays badly or a job I tolerate that pays brilliantly?
  4. Would I rather live somewhere hot forever or somewhere cold forever?
  5. Would I rather be able to talk to animals or speak every human language?
  6. Would I rather never travel again or never eat my favourite food again?
  7. Would I rather always be twenty minutes early or always five minutes late?
  8. Would I rather have one enormous friendship or ten decent ones?
  9. Would I rather know exactly when I die or exactly how?
  10. Would I rather relive my best day on loop or wipe my worst one entirely?
  11. Would I rather win an argument or keep the peace?
  12. Would I rather be the funniest person in the room or the smartest?

5. Memories and history

The day-one friends are about to run the table. These questions test shared past, which means anyone who arrived late is guessing blind — and that's fine, because the resulting score gap is honest. If you're building a quiz for a mixed group, use these sparingly or your newest friend will finish on two out of ten and feel it. If you're building one for your oldest friends, load it up.

  1. Where did we actually meet, and did either of us like the other at first?
  2. What's the first thing I ever said to you that stuck?
  3. Which trip of ours went most spectacularly wrong?
  4. What's the stupidest thing we've ever spent money on together?
  5. Which one of my exes did you dislike most, and did you say so at the time?
  6. What's the longest we've ever gone without speaking?
  7. Which of my haircuts should never have happened?
  8. What job of mine did you think I'd quit within a month?
  9. Where was I living when we became properly close?
  10. What's the one story about me you tell other people?
  11. Which night out do we both refuse to explain to anyone?
  12. What did I look like the first time you saw me?

6. Hot takes and opinions

Opinions stick in people's memory far better than facts do. Your friends might forget your birthday, but they will never forget the twenty minutes you spent explaining why a beloved film is bad. That makes hot takes brilliant quiz material — the answer is specific, it's memorable, and getting it wrong is instantly funny. Pick the opinions you've actually defended out loud.

  1. Which universally loved film do I think is overrated?
  2. What's my take on pineapple on pizza, and how strongly do I hold it?
  3. Do I think the book is always better than the film?
  4. Which popular musician do I refuse to be normal about?
  5. What's my position on socks in bed?
  6. Do I think tipping culture is fine or broken?
  7. Which food do I think is a scam?
  8. Do I skip the credits or sit through all of them?
  9. What's the hill I'll die on about how to load a dishwasher?
  10. Do I believe in astrology, tolerate it, or roll my eyes?
  11. Which "classic" do I think is genuinely boring?
  12. Is a hot dog a sandwich, according to me?

7. The ridiculous ones

Pure chaos, and absolutely necessary. Every quiz needs a couple of questions that exist only to make the group chat scream. These also do quiet structural work: they break up the intensity of the deep cuts and give people a laugh before you ask them something sharp. The best ridiculous questions still have one real answer — the comedy comes from the fact that a true friend knows it.

  1. How many days could I survive in a forest with no phone?
  2. Which animal would win in a fight against me, honestly?
  3. What would my villain origin story be?
  4. If I got one item through customs unquestioned, what am I smuggling?
  5. What's my karaoke song, and how bad is it?
  6. Which conspiracy theory could I be talked into after two drinks?
  7. What would be written on my gravestone if you wrote it out of spite?
  8. If I were a kitchen appliance, which one and why?
  9. How would I die in a horror film — first, last, or the annoying one?
  10. What's the most useless skill I'm weirdly good at?
  11. Which fictional character do I insist I'm like, and which one am I really?
  12. If I had to fight one historical figure, who am I picking?
  13. What's my order at 3am with zero judgement involved?

8. The ones that actually sting

Save these for the end. They're honest, slightly uncomfortable, and they make the best closers because the answer is never a joke. There's a real risk here — asking someone what your worst quality is invites a true answer — so only use these with people you trust to be kind about it. Done right, this is the section people remember weeks later.

  1. What's my worst quality, and don't dress it up?
  2. Am I a good listener or do I just wait for my turn?
  3. What do I do that quietly annoys you?
  4. Which of my friendships do you think isn't good for me?
  5. Am I the funny one, the sensible one, or the problem?
  6. What advice have I given you that I completely fail to follow myself?
  7. Do I ask about your life as much as you ask about mine?
  8. What have I been avoiding that you can see clearly?
  9. Am I honest with you, or just agreeable?
  10. What would you change about how I treat myself?
  11. If I disappeared for a year, what would you miss and what would you not?
  12. Do you think I'm happy right now?

How to turn these into a quiz

You only get ten, so choose like it. The single most important rule: every question you pick must have one clear answer that you can commit to before anyone guesses. If you'd hesitate between two of your own options, cut it — a question you can't answer confidently isn't a test of your friends, it's a coin flip.

Then mix the difficulty deliberately. Ten deep cuts and everyone finishes on three out of ten and feels bad; ten easy favourites and everyone ties on nine and nothing is revealed. Aim for roughly four easy ones, four that need real familiarity, and two that only a genuine day-one friend gets. That spread is what makes the leaderboard interesting — you want your scores strung out from two to nine, not clustered in the middle.

Cut anything a stranger could reason their way into. If someone who has never met you could look at the four options and pick correctly through pure logic, the question is dead weight. And this is the part most people skip: the wrong answers matter as much as the right one. A question is only good if the wrong options are tempting — plausible, in-character, things you might genuinely have said. Three obviously silly options and one real one isn't a question, it's a gift. Make your friends actually hesitate, and then watch them get it wrong.
