How Well Do You Know Me: 90 Questions for Couples

Couples are the most confident and the most wrong. It makes sense: you share an address, a calendar, a mattress and a streaming password, so you assume you've absorbed each other by now. But what you've absorbed is mostly patterns — the shape of how someone moves through a Tuesday — and patterns aren't preferences. You know your partner reaches for the same mug every morning. You may have no idea whether they even like it. The questions that catch people out are almost never the romantic ones; nobody fails "how did we meet". It's the small, specific, unglamorous things you've simply never had a reason to say out loud. Ninety of them are below, written in your voice — the one being guessed about.

1. The ones they should definitely know

This is the baseline, and it's meant to be passable. These are the facts that surface naturally if you've been paying attention at all — nothing here requires detective work, only presence. If your partner misses three or four of these, that's not a scoring problem, that's the whole conversation, and it's worth having gently rather than triumphantly. Start here so the quiz opens warm.

  1. What's my exact coffee or tea order, milk and sugar included?
  2. Which side of the bed is mine, and would I actually mind swapping?
  3. What's my shoe size, to the nearest half?
  4. Name one of my grandparents.
  5. What was my first proper job, the one with a payslip?
  6. Which of my friends would I call first with good news?
  7. What am I allergic to, or what do I just claim to be allergic to?
  8. What time do I actually fall asleep, not the time I get into bed?
  9. What's the one household job I will never do without being asked twice?
  10. What do I do for work, in a sentence I'd agree with?
  11. Which family member do I find hardest, and why?
  12. What's my order at the takeaway we use most?
  13. What's the last thing I bought that I felt slightly guilty about?

2. Daily life and habits

Only a live-in witness can answer these, which is exactly what makes them satisfying. Habits are invisible to the person who has them and glaringly obvious to the person on the sofa opposite. The catch is that noticing a habit and knowing what it means are two different skills, and this section quietly tests the second one.

  1. How many times do I hit snooze before I'm genuinely awake?
  2. What's the first thing I touch in the morning — phone, kettle, or you?
  3. Do I shower at night or in the morning, and have I ever changed?
  4. Which drawer or shelf in this house is entirely my mess?
  5. What do I do with my hands when I'm anxious about something?
  6. How long can I leave a suitcase unpacked after a trip?
  7. What noise do I make that I'm completely unaware of?
  8. Do I check the weather before leaving, or do I gamble every time?
  9. What's my sign that I'm about to be in a bad mood?
  10. Which chore do I secretly enjoy?
  11. How do I load a dishwasher, and how strongly do I feel about it?
  12. What's on my phone screen at 11pm on a normal weeknight?
  13. What do I always say I'll do at the weekend and never do?

3. Our history

Every couple has a shared timeline, and every couple's shared timeline has two slightly different versions. Memory doesn't just fade, it edits — you'll each be certain about details that flatly contradict each other. That's not a failure of love, it's how brains work, and finding the divergence is genuinely one of the funniest things you can do together.

  1. What was I wearing the first time you saw me — and be honest about how sure you are?
  2. Who made the first move, according to me?
  3. What did I actually think of you on the first date?
  4. Where did we go on our first proper trip together?
  5. What was the first meal I ever cooked for you?
  6. Which argument of ours do I still think I was right about?
  7. What's the first gift you gave me that I still have?
  8. How long was it before I told my friends about you?
  9. Which of our old flats or houses do I miss most?
  10. What's the trip of ours that went most spectacularly wrong?
  11. What song was playing at some moment I've never let you forget?
  12. Which of my exes did you meet, and what did I say about them afterwards?
  13. What's the story about us I tell at parties?

4. Tastes and preferences

This is where confident partners fall apart. Preferences are specific, checkable and almost never discussed directly — you've watched a hundred films together without ever asking which one I'd choose. If you want a section that produces a real, honest score spread rather than a polite tie, load up here.

  1. Which film would I put on if you gave me the remote and no negotiation?
  2. What's my genuine favourite thing you cook?
  3. What's the thing you cook that I quietly tolerate?
  4. Which of my own outfits do I feel best in?
  5. What's my ideal Saturday, hour by hour?
  6. Beach, mountains or city — and what's my second choice?
  7. Which of my possessions would I rescue from a fire first?
  8. What flowers would I actually like, if any?
  9. Which restaurant would I pick for a birthday with no budget?
  10. What's the one gift I've received that I still think about?
  11. Do I want a surprise party or would I never forgive you?
  12. Which album can I play start to finish without skipping?
  13. What's my order of preference: sweet, salty, or sour?

5. Feelings and values

These are harder, and they should be. What someone is proud of, afraid of, or working on tends to live one layer under the everyday chat — you have to have been listening on the nights when it slipped out. Ask a few of these and you'll find out whether your partner has been listening or just present. Both are fine; they're not the same thing.

  1. What am I most proud of that I'd never bring up myself?
  2. What's the compliment I don't know how to accept?
  3. What am I more afraid of than I let on?
  4. What am I actively trying to get better at right now?
  5. What does "I'm fine" mean when I say it in that particular tone?
  6. Which of my parents am I more like, and how do I feel about that?
  7. What would I do with a year off and no money worries?
  8. What kind of tired am I, most of the time?
  9. What's the thing I say I don't care about but obviously do?
  10. Which decision from my twenties do I still turn over?
  11. What makes me feel loved that has nothing to do with words?
  12. What did I want to be at ten, and how far did I get?
  13. What do I think people get wrong about me on first meeting?

6. The flirty ones

Every quiz needs a run of questions that make one of you laugh out loud and the other one smug. These are warm rather than heavy — they break up the intensity, and they're the ones you'll both actually enjoy getting wrong. Keep the tone playful; the goal is a nudge under the table, not a cross-examination.

  1. What did I notice about you first?
  2. Which of your shirts have I quietly claimed as mine?
  3. What's my favourite photo of us, and where is it?
  4. What do you do that makes me smile when you're not looking?
  5. Which nickname of yours do I use only when nobody's around?
  6. What's the compliment I give you most often?
  7. Do I prefer being the one who reaches for your hand, or the one who gets reached for?
  8. What's my ideal date night that costs nothing?
  9. Which of your habits did I find annoying at first and now find endearing?
  10. What would I say is your best feature, and would you guess it?
  11. What's the thing you say that I repeat to other people as if I thought of it?
  12. Where would I take you for a weekend if I planned it entirely alone?
  13. What's my move when I want your attention and won't say so?

7. The ones that start a real conversation

Save these for the end, and use two or three, not twelve. They're gently uncomfortable on purpose — the kind of question you'd never raise cold, but which lands fine inside a game. If one of them opens something up, put the quiz down and have the conversation. That's not the quiz failing; that's the quiz working.

  1. What do I worry about at 2am that I've never told you?
  2. What's one thing I've asked you for more than once?
  3. Where do I want to be living in five years, honestly?
  4. What do I think we're best at as a couple?
  5. What do I think we're worst at?
  6. What's the thing I've given up that I'm not entirely at peace with?
  7. Am I happier now than I was three years ago, and do I know why?
  8. What do I need from you when I'm at my lowest — space, or company?
  9. What's an apology I still owe someone?
  10. What would I change about how we spend our weekends?
  11. What do I think you've sacrificed for me?
  12. What do I hope you'd say about me to someone who's never met me?

Before you send it

One thing worth saying plainly, because someone always needs to hear it: a low score is not a red flag. This is a game, not a compatibility test, and it has no idea whether you're good for each other. It measures one narrow thing — how much specific, stated information has passed between you — and that's a category people are routinely bad at even in relationships that work beautifully.

When your partner misses six of ten, the overwhelmingly likely explanation isn't that they don't care. It's that you have never actually said the thing out loud. You can't be expected to guess a favourite film that was never named. Most "how did you not know that?" moments are really "I never told you that", wearing a costume.

So play it for the conversation, not the number. The best version of this is the ten minutes afterwards, where one of you says "wait, since when?" and the other one explains something they've been assuming was obvious for four years. The score is the excuse. The talk is the point. If you want more raw material, our 100 questions to ask your best friend mixes in nicely — plenty of them work just as well across a kitchen table.
