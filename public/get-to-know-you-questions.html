Get-to-Know-You Questions That Aren't Boring

"What do you do?" and "where are you from?" aren't questions. They're toll booths — you pay them to get to the other side of the conversation, and nobody remembers the transaction. The trouble with standard icebreakers is that they ask for facts, and facts don't make anyone memorable. Nobody has ever walked away thinking about someone's postcode. What makes a person come into focus is a choice or a story: what they'd pick, what they did, what they'd defend. Which is where the twist comes in. Turn these into a quiz for a group that barely knows each other and nobody expects a high score — that's precisely what makes it fun. The wrong guesses are the entertainment. Everyone learns five things about each other in about three minutes, and nobody had to sit through a single round of small talk to get there.

1. Openers that aren't a toll booth

The job of an opening question is to hand someone a door they actually want to walk through. The dead standards fail because there's exactly one correct response and it's dull for both of you. These do the same social work — low stakes, easy to answer, no confession required — but they leave the answerer somewhere interesting. Notice that each one still has a specific answer someone could guess at, because that's what makes them work as quiz material rather than conversation filler.

  1. What's the last thing I looked up on my phone that I'd be slightly embarrassed to read aloud?
  2. What's my honest opinion of the town I grew up in?
  3. If you gave me a free Saturday and no plans, what would I actually do with it?
  4. What am I unreasonably good at that has never once helped my career?
  5. What's the app I open most, and what's the app I'd claim I open most?
  6. What did I want to be when I was eight?
  7. What's the most recent thing I bought that I didn't need?
  8. Am I a morning person, a night person, or permanently exhausted?
  9. What's my order at a place I've never been to before?
  10. What's the last thing that made me laugh out loud on my own?
  11. How do I take my tea or coffee, exactly?
  12. What's the one household chore I'd happily pay someone else to do?
  13. Which of my hobbies have I quietly abandoned but still own the equipment for?
  14. What's the strangest job I've ever had?

2. Tastes that give you away

Preferences are compression. Tell me what someone rewatches when they're ill and I know more about them than a paragraph of biography would tell me. The key is precision — "what music do I like" gives you a genre and a shrug, while "which song do I skip forward to" gives you a person. These sketch someone fast, which is exactly what you need with a group that has only just met.

  1. Which film have I seen so many times I could quote the opening from memory?
  2. What's the one food I will happily eat cold, straight from the fridge, standing up?
  3. What genre do I claim to love and never actually watch?
  4. Which song would I put on to fix a bad mood?
  5. What's my seat on a plane, and how strongly do I feel about it?
  6. Which shop could I lose an entire afternoon in?
  7. What's the takeaway I order when I've given up on the day?
  8. Do I read the book, listen to the audiobook, or lie about both?
  9. Which sport can I be dragged into watching against my will?
  10. What's the drink I order when I want the night to be a good one?
  11. Which decade's clothes would I wear if I could get away with it?
  12. What's the one item in my kitchen I use constantly and everyone else finds weird?
  13. Do I want a window seat, an aisle, or the middle of the room?
  14. What's my ideal temperature, in a number, and will I argue about it?

3. Stories, not facts

Here's the difference: a fact question ends, a story question opens. Ask where someone went on holiday and you get a place name. Ask what went wrong on their worst holiday and you get ten minutes and a version of them you'd never otherwise meet. In a quiz these are gold, because the guessers are picking between four short summaries of things that might have happened to you — and then you tell the real story afterwards, which is the actual reward.

  1. What's the worst haircut I've ever paid money for?
  2. What's the most trouble I got into at school, and was it deserved?
  3. What's the closest I've come to being genuinely famous, even for ten seconds?
  4. What's the biggest thing I've ever broken by accident?
  5. What's the longest I've ever queued for something, and was it worth it?
  6. What's the story behind the scar I'd point to first?
  7. What's the most lost I've ever been?
  8. What did I do on the best birthday I've had?
  9. What's the worst thing I've ever cooked for another human being?
  10. What's the strangest thing that's ever happened to me on public transport?
  11. What's the most money I've ever wasted in one go?
  12. What's the job interview I still think about?
  13. What's the most convincing lie I told as a child?

4. Hypotheticals

Imagination is low-stakes and enormously revealing. Nobody has to admit anything true, so nobody gets defensive — and yet the answer tells you what someone finds funny, what they'd protect, and what they secretly want. That combination is rare. Hypotheticals also level the field: a stranger has a real chance of guessing these correctly by reading your personality rather than knowing your history, which is what makes them fair for a new group.

  1. If I won a large amount of money tomorrow, what's the first thing I'd buy that's purely stupid?
  2. Would I rather be able to pause time or rewind ten seconds?
  3. If I had to give a talk with no preparation, what's my topic?
  4. What's my role in a zombie apocalypse — leader, medic, or first casualty?
  5. If I could delete one everyday inconvenience from existence, what's going?
  6. Would I rather live in a city I love with no friends, or a town I hate with all of them?
  7. If I opened a shop, what would it sell?
  8. Which historical period would I visit for exactly one day?
  9. If I had to swap lives with someone in this chat for a week, who am I picking?
  10. What's the one law I'd pass if nobody could stop me?
  11. Would I rather never be cold again or never be too hot again?
  12. If I could be excellent at one thing overnight, what am I choosing?
  13. What's my reality TV show, and how long do I last?

5. Light but revealing

These are the sneaky ones. On the surface they're small — a habit, a preference, a minor confession — and yet the answers are consistently the ones people quote back at you a week later. They work because they ask about behaviour rather than beliefs. Anyone can describe the person they'd like to be; almost nobody can lie convincingly about what they actually do when the bill arrives.

  1. Do I text back immediately or three days later, and am I sorry about it?
  2. What's my move when a group can't decide where to eat?
  3. Do I keep receipts, throw them away, or find them in a coat a year later?
  4. How do I behave when I'm lost — ask, or insist I know?
  5. Am I the person who plans the trip or the person who turns up?
  6. What do I do with my hands when I'm bored in a meeting?
  7. Do I finish books I dislike out of principle?
  8. How early do I get to an airport, and how do I justify it?
  9. Do I complain to the waiter or eat the wrong meal in silence?
  10. What's the compliment I'd most like to receive from a stranger?
  11. Am I better at giving advice or taking it?
  12. What's the thing I always say I'll start on Monday?
  13. Do I remember names, faces, both, or neither?

6. Group chat chaos

Every group needs a question that eats an hour and produces nothing of value. These are those. They're not really about you — they're about giving twelve people who barely know each other something to be loudly, pointlessly opposed about, which is how strangers become a group in the first place. Keep one or two in the deck as a palate cleanser between the ones that require actual thought.

  1. Is a hot dog a sandwich, and how heated do I get about it?
  2. What's my position on pineapple on pizza, stated under oath?
  3. Do I think cereal or milk goes in the bowl first?
  4. How many people in this chat could I beat in a fight, honestly?
  5. What's the correct number of pillows, according to me?
  6. Do I fold the crisp packet or leave it as nature intended?
  7. What's my karaoke song and how much damage does it do?
  8. Which fictional character am I convinced I'm like?
  9. Do I think ghosts are real, possible, or nonsense?
  10. What's the food everyone loves that I think is a scam?
  11. How long could I last without my phone before I cracked?
  12. What's my villain origin story, in one sentence?
  13. Which of my opinions would get me thrown out of this group chat?

Using these with people who barely know you

This page is the deliberate opposite of our 100 questions for your best friend. That list rewards years of shared history — the trip that went wrong, the phrase you say constantly, the thing you're quietly proud of — and a new colleague would score two out of ten and feel it. This one is built for people who've only just met you, so the target is different: aim for questions where a thoughtful stranger has roughly a 50% shot. Someone who's paid attention for ten minutes and has decent instincts should be able to reason their way to the answer about half the time. That's the sweet spot where a correct guess feels like a small triumph and a wrong one is funny rather than embarrassing.

The wrong options carry more weight here than anywhere else. With strangers you have no shared history to work with, so the entire difficulty of the question lives in whether the three decoys are plausible. If your real karaoke song is sitting next to three obviously absurd choices, you haven't asked anything. Make every option something you might genuinely have said, and the guessers actually have to think about who you are — which is the whole point.

And set the tone before you send it. With a new group, a low score is the joke, not the verdict — nobody's friendship is on trial, because there isn't one yet. Someone finishing on three out of ten is the funniest possible outcome and should be treated that way. These also work perfectly well read aloud: at a table, on a first team call, in a car, in a kitchen at midnight with new housemates. The quiz format just gives everyone a scoreboard to be smug about afterwards. If you want more forced choices to argue over, the would you rather list pairs well with these, and the guide covers how to build a set that scores well.
